Skilled Worker Shortage in Germany:
Challenges and Solutions 2026

Back to Overview

The skilled worker shortage in Germany is no longer just an abstract prognosis, but a reality in almost all sectors of the economy. Despite economic fluctuations, the structural gap caused by demographic changes remains one of the greatest hurdles for German economic growth. In this article, we highlight the current situation and show ways for companies to attract professionals from abroad.

1. The Status Quo: Skilled Worker Shortage in Figures

According to current reports from the Institute of the German Economy (IW Cologne) and industry associations like Bitkom and VDI, there is a clear demand as of early 2026:

109,000 Open IT positions (Bitkom)
148,500 STEM gap (IW Cologne)
99,470 Open Engineering positions (VDI)
68,000 Missing Teachers (KMK)

IT and Technology: The Heart of Digitalization

Digital transformation is driving demand for specialists massively. Particularly sought after are software developers, IT security analysts, and experts in artificial intelligence. According to Bitkom, 79 % of companies expect the shortage to worsen in the future.

Engineering and the Energy Transition

Without qualified engineers, projects like the energy transition or the expansion of digital infrastructure cannot be implemented. The VDI continues to report high demand, particularly for civil engineers and specialists for renewable energies.

2. Attracting professionals from abroad

In view of the increasing shortage of skilled workers in Germany, attracting professionals from abroad is becoming increasingly important. International talent plays a decisive role in filling vacancies in fields such as IT, engineering and healthcare.

Germany has significantly improved its legal framework in recent years to facilitate the immigration of qualified labor. This includes simplified visa procedures, faster recognition of qualifications and targeted programs for the integration of international professionals.

For companies, recruiting international talent offers great opportunities: they not only secure their personnel requirements, but also strengthen innovation and competitiveness in a global market.

Advantages of international talent:

  • Closing specific qualification gaps
  • Increasing innovation through diverse perspectives
  • Strengthening global competitiveness

3. Visa and entry processes: The new Skilled Immigration Act

The modernized Skilled Immigration Act offers two central paths for access to the German labor market and makes it easier to attract skilled workers from abroad.

The Opportunity Card

Since 2024, the Opportunity Card has allowed skilled workers from non-EU countries to enter Germany to look for work. Allocation is based on a points system considering qualifications, language skills (German A1 or English B2), experience, and age. Working in Germany Visa processes have become significantly more flexible as a result.

Requirements in detail
German A1 or English B2, professional experience and a minimum age are requirements for the points system.

The EU Blue Card

For highly qualified academics and IT specialists, the EU Blue Card remains the gold standard. With adjusted salary thresholds (as of 2026: approx. €45,934 for shortage occupations), access to the German labor market for experts is easier than ever.

Current salary limits
Approx. €45,934 for shortage occupations (such as IT and engineering) in 2026.

Conclusion: Proactive Recruitment as the Key

The skilled worker shortage is an ongoing challenge. Companies that invest in international recruitment, automated onboarding processes, and an open corporate culture secure the best talents of tomorrow.
